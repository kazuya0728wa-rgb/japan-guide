🚃 How to Get Around Japan Train · IC Card · Apps
💳 IC Card (Train Card)
1Buy at any station or convenience store: Suica, ICOCA, or Pasmo — all work nationwide
2Touch the card on the IC reader at the ticket gate to enter and exit — no paper ticket needed
3Also works at convenience stores, vending machines, and some restaurants
4Recharge at any ticket machine — cash only. Keep ¥2,000+ loaded at all times
5iPhone users: Add Suica to Apple Wallet app — no physical card needed
🚉 Riding the Train (Step by Step)
1Open Google Maps, search your destination, tap "Transit" mode for step-by-step directions
2Check the line name and color — each train line has a color shown on platform signs
3Touch IC card on the reader to enter gate → board correct car → exit at your stop
4Inside: no phone calls, speak quietly. Priority seats (near doors) are for elderly and pregnant passengers
5Touch IC card at exit gate — fare deducted automatically. No need to buy tickets

🏙️ Transport Tips by City
🗼 Tokyo
Yamanote Line (green loop) connects all major areas. Metro 1-day pass ¥600 saves money if you take 4+ metro rides. IC card is always the simplest option.
⛩️ Kyoto
City Bus is the main way to get around. Buy a 1-day bus pass (¥700) at Kyoto Station — unlimited rides. Taxi from Kyoto Station to most major temples: ¥1,000–1,500.
🏯 Osaka
Osaka Metro 1-day pass (¥800) covers almost everything. JR Loop Line is faster to Osaka Castle. Dotonbori/Namba area is best explored on foot once you arrive.

Flexible 1-day plan — food, temples & city icons
Best for: First-time visitors, couples, food lovers, iconic photo spots.
☀️ Morning
09:00⚠️ Popular📸 Photo
Tsukiji Outer Market
⏱ 90 min💴 ¥800–1,500Free entry

Seafood breakfast, tamagoyaki, fresh sushi — Japan's most famous food market. The "outer market" with food stalls. (Inner market moved to Toyosu in 2018.)

💡 Pro Tip: Most stalls open 6–7am. Tamagoyaki and fresh sushi sell out before 10:30am. Go straight to Marutake for tamagoyaki (¥150–200). Sushizanmai is open 24h with English menus — great for first-timers who want to sit down.
⚡ Backup if crowded / rainy
Heavy rain: Tokyo Station basement (Gransta / Ramen Street / Ichibangai) is a dry covered food hall with no entry fee.

⏱ 2 h💴 ¥0–1,000Temple: Free

Classic Tokyo temple, old-Tokyo atmosphere, snacks and souvenirs on Nakamise. Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) is the iconic photo spot at the entrance.

💡 Pro Tip: Temple is open 24/7 but inner chamber closes ~17:00. Best photos without crowds: arrive before noon. Try ningyo-yaki cakes on Nakamise (¥200–400).
⚡ Backup if crowded / rainy
Too crowded: Walk 10 min east to Hoppy Street / Shin-Nakamise — much calmer with local food.
Rain: Take shelter in the temple's main hall (large covered space).

⏱ 2.5 h💴 ¥1,000

KYOTO

Flexible 1-day plan — shrines, temples & old streets
Best for: Culture lovers, couples, first-time Kyoto visitors.
☀️ Morning
08:00🔴 Very popularFree · 24h📸 Photo
Fushimi Inari Taisha
⏱ 1.5–2 h💴 Free

Thousands of red torii gates winding up a mountain. Walk only as far as energy allows — the first 30 min (to Yotsutsuji viewpoint) is the most photogenic.

💡 Pro Tip: At 08:00 the torii tunnel has only a few people — by 10:00 it's packed with tour groups. Kyoto Station → JR Inari: JR Nara Line, 5 min, ¥140.
⚡ Backup if crowded / rainy
Rain: The torii tunnels are covered and atmospheric in rain — actually a great photo opportunity. Waterproof shoes recommended as stone paths get slippery.
🚌 Walk 10 min to Tofukuji → Bus 207 to Kiyomizumichi · ~40–50 min · ¥230 · OR taxi ¥1,200–1,500
10:15🔴 Very popular💴 ¥500📸 Photo
Kiyomizu-dera Temple + Ninenzaka / Sannenzaka
⏱ 2 h💴 ¥500–1,500

Classic Kyoto temple with wooden stage view (no nails used). Walk down through preserved Ninenzaka and Sannenzaka streets — souvenir shops and matcha cafes.

💡 Pro Tip: Best wooden stage photo: from Okuno-in sub-temple to the left. Matcha soft cream on Sannenzaka: ¥400–600. Some sections may have ongoing restoration work — main stage is always accessible.
⚡ Backup if crowded / rainy
Streets packed: Head directly to Gion by taxi (~¥1,000). Save Ninenzaka for early morning next visit.
🌤️ Afternoon
🚶 Walk ~20–30 min (scenic route through Sannenzaka → Gion) · OR 🚌 Bus to Shijo ¥230
13:00⚠️ Popular at lunch
Nishiki Market
⏱ 60–75 min💴 ¥500–1,200

Kyoto's "kitchen" — 400m covered arcade with yuba tofu, pickles, matcha sweets, grilled octopus. Eat at shop counters. Walking while eating is considered rude.

💡 Pro Tip: Best stops: Yubahan (yuba tofu), Nishiki Warai (octopus skewer), Nishiki Wakana (matcha ice cream). Market generally open 10:00–18:00. Some shops closed Wednesdays.
🚌 Bus or 🚶 Walk ~15 min to Gion Shijo
14:30
Honke Owariya (lunch) or Gion Izuju
⏱ 60–75 min💴 ¥1,200–1,800

Honke Owariya: soba restaurant operating since 1465 — a piece of Kyoto history. Gion Izuju: Kyoto-style pressed sushi (battera) — very local taste.

💡 Pro Tip: Honke Owariya is on Teramachi-dori near Nishiki. The hiyashi soba (cold soba) is recommended in summer. Opens from 11:00.
🌆 Evening
🚶 Walk ~15–25 min to Gion / Hanamikoji
16:00📸 Photo
Gion District + Pontocho Alley
⏱ 2 h💴 Free walking

Hanamikoji Street, Shimbashi historic teahouse district, then lantern-lit Pontocho alley at dusk. Best geiko/maiko spotting time: 17:30–18:30 near Hanamikoji.

💡 Pro Tip: Observe geiko/maiko from distance — no touching, no flash. Pontocho riverside terraces open May–Sep: magical dinner experience. Yasaka Shrine (free, always open) at east end is beautiful at dusk.
18:30📅 Reservation recommended
Menbaka Fire Ramen or Pontocho dinner
⏱ 60–75 min💴 ¥1,200–3,000

Menbaka: theatrical ramen where burning scallion oil is poured tableside — dramatic and memorable. Or pick any restaurant in Pontocho alley with English menu visible.

💡 Pro Tip: Menbaka opens ~17:30, limited seats — book ahead at their website. The fire only happens if you add the scallion oil. Very popular for photos/video.

Nishiki Market — Full Food Tour
⏱ 2 h💴 ¥1,000–2,500

💡 Route: Enter from east end → Murakami-ju (pickles) → Yubahan (yuba) → Nishiki Warai (grilled octopus) → Nishiki Wakana (matcha ice cream) → exit west. Budget ¥1,500–2,000 for a full tasting tour.

Hisago Restaurant — Kyoto Oyakodon
⏱ 60 min💴 ¥1,000–1,200

Famous for oyakodon (chicken-egg rice bowl) using dashi made from kyoto-grown ingredients. One of Gion's most beloved lunch spots. Queue expected but moves fast.

💡 Pro Tip: Opens at 11:30. Arrive at 11:15 to avoid the worst of the queue. Cash only. The oyakodon is subtle and delicate — very different from Tokyo versions.

13:30
Gion Tsujiri — Matcha Parfait
⏱ 45 min💴 ¥900–1,400

Kyoto's most famous matcha parfait — made with ceremonial-grade matcha and Kyoto-sourced toppings. The gold standard for matcha desserts in Japan.

💡 Pro Tip: Queue can be 30–60 min on weekends. Order the Chagyu Parfait (¥1,300) — it has matcha ice cream, warabi mochi, red bean, and chestnuts. Takeout is faster than sit-down.

Arashiyama Food Walk — Tofu, Mochi, Green Tea
⏱ 90 min💴 ¥500–1,500

Arashiyama's main street is lined with unique Kyoto food shops — fu no mise (gluten), warabi mochi, fresh yudofu. The area has foods you won't find anywhere else in Japan.

💡 Pro Tip: Nakamura-ya for warabi mochi (soft rice cake in kinako powder, ¥300–500). Shoraian for yudofu (simmered tofu) in a bamboo garden setting (¥2,000–3,000 full set).
🌆 Evening
18:30
Pontocho Alley — Dinner
⏱ 90 min💴 ¥2,000–5,000

The atmospheric narrow alley along the Kamo River — lantern-lit restaurants with river terrace seating (May–Sep). The ultimate Kyoto evening dining experience.

💡 Pro Tip: Look for restaurants with plastic food models and visible English menus. Expect ¥3,000–5,000 for a proper dinner on a terrace. Alternatively, Kappa Sushi or Tofu Cuisine Sagano for a more budget-friendly traditional meal.
